Safety Defects and Fuel Efficiency in the Automotive Industry

with George Ball, Gilvan Souza. 2026. Production and Operations Management. (Forthcoming)

  • Finalist, POMS College of Sustainable Operations Best Student Paper Competition 2023
  • SSRN
Abstract

Automotive manufacturers have been under intense pressure to improve the fuel efficiency, or miles per gallon (MPG), of their internal combustion engine (ICE) vehicles. Research that explores hidden costs of continuing to push the limits of ICE MPG is sparse. We analyze the relationship between the MPG of 643 unique model-engines produced and sold by 18 manufacturers over an 18-year period and 19,785 vehicle safety defects reported to the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA). Each additional MPG improvement is associated with a 17.5 percent increase in safety defects. Results are confirmed when using fuel prices as an instrument for MPG. In post-hoc analyses, we find that a 2011 federal government fuel efficiency policy change explains an increase in the adoption of MPG-enhancing engine technologies, which subsequently led to an increase in safety defects. Moreover, we find that an increase in MPG is associated with an increase in safety recalls, and that safety defects mediate the relationship between MPG and safety recalls. To address selection bias concerns, we confirm our results using a separate quality reporting channel, demonstrating that increases in MPG are associated with a reduction in JD Power Quality & Reliability ratings. We also examine a key alternative explanation, that increasing MPG causes vehicles to be driven more miles, inherently leading to more defects. No evidence is found for this alternative; in fact we find the opposite to be true: an increase in MPG is associated with lower mileage at the time of the defect, indicating that increasing MPG leads to both more and faster defects. For manufacturers, our results identify an unexpected downside to striving to meet aggressive, federally mandated fuel efficiency standards: less safe vehicles. For NHTSA, our findings identify a possible contradiction in their two key objectives: sustainability and safety. Our study leads to policy recommendations, which we were fortunate to present to NHTSA’s Associate Administrator for Vehicle Safety Research and members of his staff.

Got Organic Milk? Joint Inventory Model with Supply Uncertainties and Partial Substitution

with Michael Lim, Zhenkang Peng, Ying Rong. Operations Research Letters, 49(5), 2021.

  • Winner, Best Paper Award 2021, KOPOMS (Korea Production and Operations Management Society) Spring Conference
  • SSRN
Abstract

We study the joint inventory problem for two partially substitutable (organic and regular) products with different levels of supply reliability. Increasing substitution rate results in a decreased organic order but an increased regular order, as the regular product becomes an effective substitute during the organic product's stock-out. Higher disruption risk increases the order size of both products, but higher yield uncertainty only increases the organic order size whereas the regular order size decreases or remains unchanged.

From Worker Engagement with Energy Management Systems to Energy Efficiency

with Amrou Awaysheh, Christopher Chen, Owen Wu. 2026. Under Review at Manufacturing & Service Operations Management.

  • Presented at Early-Career Sustainable Operations Management Workshop 2025
  • SSRN
Abstract

Problem definition: Energy Management Systems (EnMS) offer manufacturers substantial data on energy cost and consumption patterns. However, the realization of potential energy efficiency (EE) gains is closely tied to human engagement with these systems. This study investigates how distinct dimensions of worker engagement with EnMS relate to EE in global manufacturing operations. Methodology/results: Drawing on site-month data from a global conglomerate spanning 2017-2022, we examine four dimensions of worker engagement: frequency (sessions), breadth (workers), volume (actions), and attention (time). We find a significant positive relationship between worker engagement with EnMS and overall EE. We also find evidence for a nonlinear association of EnMS engagement with EE. Notably, we find that more frequent, quick monitoring engagement shows a strong positive association with contemporaneous EE. Interestingly, longer configuration-oriented engagement is associated with delayed EE, partly by facilitating subsequent quick monitoring engagement. Additionally, we find that engineering teams and line operators—those in direct control of machinery and day-to-day processes—are most strongly associated with converting energy insights into tangible energy savings. Managerial implications: Our findings highlight the importance of worker engagement as a key correlate of realized energy efficiency. By quantifying the nuanced associations between worker engagement patterns and EE, this study offers actionable insights for managers to optimize EnMS utilization and enhance EE in manufacturing operations.

Operational Workload and Safety Risk: Evidence from Industrial Equipment Use in Material
Handling Operations

with Mateus Lima, Zach Wright, Diwas KC. 2026. To be Submitted to Manufacturing & Service Operations Management.

  • SSRN
Abstract

Problem definition: Using a unique proprietary dataset generated by Industrial Internet of Things (IIoT) sensors embedded in material-handling equipment and linked to operational logs, we investigate how workload affects safety risk in material handling operations (MHO). We examine two operational levers that may shape this relationship: task complexity (proxied by lifting intensity) and pre-work safety checklist completions. Specifically, we ask: (i) How does workload affect safety risk? (ii) Does task complexity amplify the effect of workload on safety risk? and (iii) Do pre-work safety checklist completions help mitigate the effect of workload on safety risk? Methodology/results: We partnered with an IIoT technology provider for MHO, obtaining shift-level data from ten U.S. facilities spanning 2019-2024. Using fixed-effects models and an instrumental variable approach, we find that workload is positively associated with safety risk. We also find that shifts with greater lifting intensity, our proxy for task complexity, exhibit higher safety risk; however, lifting intensity does not amplify the effect of workload on safety risk. In contrast, completing safety checklists attenuates the negative consequences of workload on safety risk. We find evidence of a concave-increasing pattern in the relationship between workload and safety risk. We also find that completing safety checklists mitigates the association of workload with safety risk for low-severity impacts, as opposed to higher-severity impacts. Managerial implications: Our findings highlight workload as an operational driver of safety risk in MHO. We find that safety risk does not appear only after workers reach high workload levels; it begins to rise much earlier, especially when workload moves from low to moderate levels. We also highlight that pre-work safety checklists are a useful, low-cost safety intervention under higher workload, but attenuate the workload–risk association only for routine, low-severity impacts. Thus, safety checklists should complement broader operational controls such as congestion management, speed control, staffing, and task pacing.
